There is a question I have been circling for the last four years. First as a talk inside McKinsey, then as a personal essay, now as the thing I quietly look for in every product I touch. It is not "is this accessible." That question is too easy, and the answer is almost always no.

The real question is this: _whose problem does this product think disability is?_

The answer tells you everything. It tells you where engineering effort goes, which users are treated as default and which are treated as exception, and which users an AI feature will quietly fail for.

## The five models in brief

**Medical.** Disability is a problem inside the person.

**Social.** Disability is produced by an environment that assumes too narrow a user.

**Cultural.** Disability is identity and community, not only limitation.

**Economic.** Disability is defined by access to labor and institutions.

**Human-variation.** People vary across many dimensions. Disability appears when systems are too rigid for that variation.

> The model you pick determines the product you build. It determines defaults, telemetry, and roadmaps.

## How to identify the model in a product

Look at settings. If accessibility is hidden as a special case, the product usually operates from a medical model.

Look at onboarding. Asking about preferred interaction patterns usually reflects a human-variation model. Asking only whether someone "has a disability" tends to preserve exception thinking.

Look at AI responses. If disability is always described as someone else's problem, your model assumptions are leaking through the system.

## Why this matters more in AI products

In deterministic interfaces, accessibility can be audited page by page. In AI products, output is variable and constantly shifting. Accessibility has to live in architecture, not remediation.

That means prompts, evaluations, defaults, and component scaffolds must encode inclusive behavior.

## The leverage point

For years, organizations framed accessibility as a slower path. AI-assisted development can invert that constraint. When assistants generate robust semantics, focus behavior, and clear labels by default, the cost argument collapses.

That is the work now: making inclusive engineering the default path under real delivery pressure.
