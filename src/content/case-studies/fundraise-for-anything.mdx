## The bet

When Crowdrise's co-founder and CPO reached out to me directly on LinkedIn, the
company had a clear thesis and a clear gap. The thesis: their next big feature —
**Fundraise for Anything**, which let people raise money for any
cause rather than only registered 501(c)(3) charities — would succeed or fail on
mobile, because the giving it enabled was spontaneous, social, and happened on
the phone in someone's hand. The gap: the entire site was non-responsive, and
there was no one to fix it. I was hired as Crowdrise's first frontend engineer to
close it.

On my first day I joined a call with the CPO, the CTO, and the lead designer, who
walked me through the FFA designs so far and asked for honest feedback. I gave it.
The candor on day one surprised them — and it set the tenor for how I'd work there:
with no product manager on staff, I ended up leading across design and engineering
to ship the thing, translating the CPO's product vision into what actually got
built.

## What I walked into

The starting landscape was bleak in the specific way that legacy consumer products
are bleak. All the frontend had been written by backend developers, so there was no
frontend tooling of any kind — no build step, no conventions. There was a single
enormous CSS file and hundreds of PHP templates spread across several disparate
backend systems, the residue of multiple abandoned attempts to reorganize the
codebase. My first real task wasn't writing code; it was reading it — mapping what
existed, and finding pages I could delete outright because nothing pointed at them
anymore.

I didn't want to add yet another system on top of that mess, but there was no clean
way to retrofit responsiveness into the existing templates without destabilizing
everything. So I introduced a parallel `responsive_index.php` entry point
for the pages I converted, and started with the global furniture: header, footer, and
modals. We redesigned those responsively first, then built a retrofit so the
*old* non-responsive pages inherited the new navigation too — meaning a user
moving between a converted page and a legacy one never felt the seam. I chose
**Foundation** as the framework to move quickly, and worked closely with
our designer to define a reusable component system, which let me convert many pages
on my own without redesigning each from scratch.

## The hard part: hundreds of pages I wasn't allowed to break

The fundraiser page — the one page that mattered most for FFA — was also the one I
couldn't simply make responsive in place. Here's why.

Crowdrise's business ran on partnerships: major marathons like New York and Boston,
charities like the Red Cross, celebrities like Seth Rogen and Kristen Bell. To win
and keep those partners, the marketing team could deeply customize a partner's
fundraiser page through a freeform HTML/CSS field in the admin tools — custom colors,
imagery, sometimes bespoke functionality. Crucially, **that markup was written
by marketing, never by engineering**, and over the years it had accumulated
into hundreds of pages, some of them completely unrecognizable, some re-styling the
very header and footer I was trying to standardize.

That's a genuinely hard constraint, and not a technical one I could refactor away:
any responsive change to the shared fundraiser template risked silently breaking
hundreds of pages of arbitrary, hand-injected markup that no engineer owned or could
fully audit. Rewriting in place would have been reckless. So the right call was to
**fork a new, dedicated responsive fundraiser page just for FFA** —
isolating the new flow so it could be modern and mobile-first without putting years of
partner customization at risk. Forking wasn't the shortcut; it was the disciplined
choice given a surface that non-engineers had been writing into for years.

The FFA fundraiser flow itself needed real product care, not just responsive markup.
Creating an FFA fundraiser was a different journey from the existing charity flow: it
required more identity verification (KYC), and that extra friction had to be sequenced
so it informed rather than overwhelmed someone in the middle of an emotional, urgent
impulse to help. Getting that funnel to convert on a small screen was the work.

## The proof, on the ground

To launch FFA, Crowdrise ran a promotion called the **24-Hour Impact Project**:
we sent activist Mallory Brown to places that needed help — Haiti, Detroit, Nepal,
Ethiopia, and more — and ran a 24-hour FFA fundraiser for each. Mallory was on the
ground the whole time, watching the donations come in on the fundraiser page and
spending the money in real time so donors could see their impact almost immediately.

## What it added up to

FFA worked. Within its first year the feature grew into roughly a fifth of Crowdrise's
business, and it was central to the story that took the company through a Series A and,
ultimately, to its acquisition by GoFundMe. I want to be precise about my part in that:
I didn't invent the feature or close the round. I built the responsive fundraiser
experience that FFA depended on, set the frontend standards the team built on afterward,
and made the mobile bet executable. Leadership was right that mobile would decide it. My
job was to make sure the product was actually there when the phone came out — and it was.

> The medium was the message. A feature built for spontaneous, social, on-the-ground
> giving had to work on the device people actually had in their hands — not the
> idealized desktop user, but the real person, in the real moment.

That instinct — build for the real conditions of real people, not the convenient
average — is the same one that drives my accessibility work today. Responsive design
and inclusive design are the same discipline wearing different clothes: both refuse the
fiction of a single "normal" user, and both make the product better for everyone when
you design for the person at the edge. The phone in Haiti and the screen reader in a
code editor are the same problem. I've been solving it for a decade.
